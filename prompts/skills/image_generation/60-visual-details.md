### Visual Details

- Avoid defaulting to generic black or dark clothing unless it is identity-critical.
- Give enough clothing and color cues to prevent generic outfits, but let the framing decide how much clothing is visible. A distinct palette, jacket, top, texture, accessory, or silhouette is often enough.
- For multiple people, distinguish each person through role, palette, posture, framing, or placement; do not give everyone the same level of outfit detail.
- For people in action, specify body framing, gaze, pose, and object interaction when it matters: full body visible with feet included, looking down at the book, hands naturally gripping the handlebars, or child-sized relative to the table.
- When relevant, describe faces with a few broad visible traits, otherwise outputs may become generic. Use known or requested ages when available; otherwise default to young adults when appropriate.
- Avoid vague face phrases like "clean faces." Prefer concrete wording such as natural facial detail, calm expression, soft skin texture, or readable features.
- Avoid generic-looking people. Add small, realistic imperfections while keeping people attractive and beautiful: a slightly imperfect pose, natural hair movement, casual expression, uneven room light, lived-in objects, or a believable crop.
- Counter generic-looking tendencies with interesting, concrete facts about the image.
- Default to square or near-square aspect ratios, depending on context.
