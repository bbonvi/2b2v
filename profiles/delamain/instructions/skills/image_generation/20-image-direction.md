# Image Direction

Choose a specific visual direction before adding details: intended use, medium, mood, focal hierarchy, composition, camera treatment, lighting, palette, and environment. Use one dominant style and one rendering or camera treatment; add another style term only when it contributes distinct information such as era, material, lens, or finish. Concrete art direction is preferable to generic requests for something polished, cinematic, detailed, or beautiful.

Treat image creation as literal and comparatively weak at spatial reasoning. Every named object, trait, action, and reference may appear in frame. Mention only what should be visible from the chosen viewpoint. Convey hidden context through visible evidence such as posture, gaze, reflections, shadows, props, weather, wear, reactions, or environmental changes.

For complex scenes, establish one focal subject or cluster, a few secondary elements, and quieter supporting areas. Concentrate the sharpest edges, strongest contrast, richest texture, and most detailed rendering around the focal area. Let backgrounds and secondary subjects be softer, simpler, lower-contrast, partly cropped, or farther away. Do not distribute equal emphasis everywhere.

Describe spatial relationships explicitly when they matter: foreground and background, left and right, distance, orientation, overlap, and visible separating space. For multiple people, distinguish them by role, palette, silhouette, posture, placement, or scale. Avoid flattening groups into a crowded row unless that is the requested design.

For realistic images, favor believable light, natural softness, restrained detail, plausible materials, relaxed framing, and small real-world imperfections. Avoid the default synthetic look of uniform sharpness, glossy skin, excessive micro-detail, and overprocessed contrast unless the request specifically calls for it. For stylized work, likewise vary detail density so the result feels intentionally designed rather than uniformly packed.

Make materials and surfaces concrete when they matter: brushed metal, frosted glass, worn paper, soft knit fabric, glossy enamel, rough stone, ink wash, low-poly geometry, or translucent plastic. Translate abstract moods into observable cues such as color temperature, spacing, posture, expression, depth, weather, and light direction.

Treat the background as designed support, not a list of decorations. State its role, depth, quietness, and contrast before adding props. Do not force every requested fact into frame; select the few visual cues that make the subject recognizable and the composition coherent.

Use-case requirements:

- Ads and product work: define the product, audience, concept, focal arrangement, material treatment, copy if any, and a deliberate callout area.
- UI mockups: define screen type, canvas, hierarchy, realistic labels or data, spacing, typography, and interaction state.
- Infographics and diagrams: define audience, learning objective, labels, arrows, sequence, and simplification level.
- Posters, covers, and slides: define title area, focal image, subject scale, crop, hierarchy, negative space, and quiet supporting regions.
- Logos and marks: request one original, scalable concept with a clear silhouette, simple shapes, balanced negative space, a plain presentation background, and only a few brand cues.
- Sequential images: define panel count and layout, with one readable action or story beat per panel.

Before submitting, silently check that the brief has a clear focal point, coherent viewpoint, deliberate style, sufficient identity cues, and no contradictory or invisible requirements. Prefer one strong visual decision over a pile of fashionable adjectives.
